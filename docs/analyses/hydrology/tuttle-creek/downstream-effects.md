# üìò 0. Executive Summary

The **Big Blue River corridor downstream of Tuttle Creek Dam** represents one of the most  
dynamic‚Äîand sensitive‚Äîhydrologic environments in Kansas.

Downstream effects arise from:

- **Reservoir operations** (flood releases, re-regulation, low-flow augmentation)  
- **Sediment trapping**, creating sediment-starved reaches  
- **Temperature and DO shifts** from hypolimnetic releases  
- **Nutrient load transformations**  
- **Geomorphic adjustments** to altered flow regimes  
- **Biological responses** (mussels, fish assemblages, macroinvertebrates)  
- **WID 2025** sediment-pulse and turbidity propagation  
- **Climate-driven hydrologic changes** affecting future downstream conditions  

This file consolidates the complete statewide-to-local analysis of downstream effects for KFM.

# üìâ 2. Hydrologic Alterations from Reservoir Regulation

### üîß Mechanisms
- **Flood control releases** reshape downstream stage/frequency  
- **Sediment-starved water** promotes channel incision  
- **Thermal regime shifts** due to hypolimnetic cold-water releases  
- **Reduced flow variability** disrupts ecological cues  
- **Baseflow augmentation** during drought stabilizes flows  
- **Peak attenuation** alters floodplain connectivity  

### üìà Key Observed Impacts (1962‚Äì2025)
- Deepening of primary thalweg  
- Loss of side-channel habitats  
- Increased bank armoring  
- Greater susceptibility to channel narrowing  
- Seasonal DO minima from cold-water release density wedges

# üü´ 3. Sediment-Starvation & Geomorphic Response

### Why sediment starvation occurs
Tuttle Creek traps **>95%** of incoming sediment.

### Resulting geomorphic signatures
- **Bed incision** (multi-decadal)  
- **Coarsening of substrate** (gravel replacing fine sand)  
- **Reduced turbidity baseline**  
- **Bank instability** during high flows  
- **Loss of depositional bars**

### ASCII Sediment Wave Diagram
```text
Upstream (sediment-rich) ‚Üí Reservoir trap ‚Üí Sediment-poor water ‚Üí Incision + channel armoring
```

### Flood-year exceptions
- 1993 and 2019 floods briefly reversed incision trends  
- Introduced fine material into tailwater zone  
- Provided temporary habitat rejuvenation

# üå´Ô∏è 4. WID (2025) Downstream Turbidity & Sediment Dynamics

The WID demonstration introduced the first **engineered sediment release** in Tuttle Creek history.

### 4.1 Density-Current Pulse at the Dam
- Fluidized sediment creates a **near-bed density plume**  
- Plume exits via outlet works depending on gate settings  
- Rapid drop in velocity immediately downstream (<500 m)  

### 4.2 Turbidity Propagation
- NTU increases decaying approximately **exponentially** with distance  
- 1‚Äì5 mile zone experiences moderate turbidity elevation  
- 5‚Äì15 mile zone: values fall back to background within hours  

### 4.3 Biological Safeguards
- DO monitored at 1‚Äì5 minute intervals  
- Mussel beds surveyed pre- and post-WID  
- No long-term WID-related impairment expected with proper operational thresholds

# üå± 5. Ecological Impacts

## üêö 5.1 Mussels (Unionids)
Highly sensitive to:
- Suspended sediment  
- Hypoxia  
- Substrate coarsening  

Tuttle Creek downstream corridor contains:
- Pocketbook, pimpleback, black sandshell, and rare species occurrences  

### WID interactions
- Turbidity pulses can reduce feeding efficiency  
- Short pulses (<6 hours) generally recoverable  
- DO sags are the primary risk  

---

## üêü 5.2 Fish Assemblages

### Typical downstream assemblage
- Channel catfish  
- Sauger  
- Paddlefish (downstream corridor)  
- Drum  
- Red shiner  
- Orangespotted sunfish  

### Hydrologic alteration impacts
- Disrupted spawning cues  
- Reduction in shallow-water refugia  
- Changes in baseflow create competitive shifts  

---

## üêõ 5.3 Macroinvertebrates

Indicators of:
- Water-quality stress  
- Substrate scouring  
- Organic matter transport  

Reservoir-regulated reaches show:
- Reduced EPT richness  
- Dominance of tolerant taxa  

WID 2025:
- Minor temporary increases in fine particles  
- Rapid post-event recovery expected

# üå°Ô∏è 6. Water-Quality Effects

## 6.1 Temperature Regime
- Cold hypolimnetic releases modify seasonal cues  
- WID does not significantly alter thermal pattern  

## 6.2 Dissolved Oxygen
- DO sag risk greatest in tailwater  
- Monitoring design places high-frequency sondes at B1/B2/B3 stations  

## 6.3 Nutrients
- Reservoir processes ‚Üí nutrient-stripped water  
- Downstream: increased sensitivity to nutrient pulses  
- WID contributes minimal nutrient load relative to background
